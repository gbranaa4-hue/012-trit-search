TITLE:
When does weighted combination beat majority voting? A six-study scoping
rule, ending in a designed-to-kill test

DESCRIPTION  (paste this whole block)
----------------------------------------------------------------
Combining several unreliable binary judgments admits two classical
answers: unweighted majority voting, and reliability-weighted (log-odds)
combination. Weighted combination is provably optimal when reliabilities
are known; voting is the folk-robust default when they are not. This
preprint reports a six-study empirical program that sharpens the folklore
into a falsifiable scoping rule.

Two studies against a real game's live decision logic first located both
sides of the boundary: a vote-of-three beats an information-poor OR-gate
(+1.8pp, 30/30 seeds), and the same vote loses decisively to an
information-rich linear threshold (-4.6pp, 30/30 seeds), because
discretizing continuous evidence discards margin information. Three
independent-field checks then tested the rule where each field's own
theory makes a prediction: spiking-neuron population decoding (weighted
wins clean; voting wins by +37pp under per-trial outlier contamination),
classical triple-modular redundancy (weighted wins under stable
calibration; the gap collapses under reliability drift), and scikit-learn
ensembles under covariate shift (weak and ambiguous — the shift degraded
accuracy without reordering classifier reliability). That weak result
refined the rule into its final form: weighted combination beats voting
exactly as long as the relative reliability RANKING learned at
calibration time still holds at decision time; it degrades in proportion
to rank reordering, not absolute miscalibration.

A final experiment was designed to kill this refinement and failed to: a
covariate shift engineered (on validation data only) to flip the
previously-best classifier to worst reverses the weighted decoder's
advantage (majority wins, paired t=-2.7), while a damage-matched,
rank-preserving shift from the same shift family leaves weighted
combination winning (contrast t=+2.6), with a pooled dose-response
between rank preservation and the weighted advantage of rho=+0.44
(p=3.9e-7).

All experiments pre-registered their predictions in the code before
running; negative and weak results are reported alongside positive ones.
Complete reproducible code is public (github.com/gbranaa4-hue/012-trit-search
and github.com/gbranaa4-hue/Spikeling).
